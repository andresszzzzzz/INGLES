Digital Harassment in the Age of Hyperconnectivity: The Impact of Cyberbullying on Teen Mental Health.

Context (Social and Technological)

The problem takes place in the social and school environment of adolescents (ages 12 to 17), a demographic group that spends an average of 4 to 6 hours a day on screens. After accelerated digitalization in recent years, social interactions migrated massively to platforms like TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, and WhatsApp groups. The anonymity enabled by networks, immediate virality, and the lack of adult supervision in digital spaces have transformed socialization dynamics, turning the internet into fertile ground for harassment 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

Affected Population

  • Directly affected: Adolescent victims of harassment, who suffer public exposure of their private lives, mockery, or digital exclusion.
  • Indirectly affected: Perpetrators (who often normalize digital violence), observers/witnesses (peers who stay silent out of fear), and families and school environments, who often learn of the situation only after serious consequences have already occurred.

Why the Problem Matters (Justification)

Unlike traditional school bullying, cyberbullying does not end when the school bell rings; it follows the victim into their own room. The psychological consequences are devastating and include a drastic drop in academic performance, social isolation, sleep disorders, severe anxiety, depression, and in the most critical cases, suicidal ideation. Because adolescence is a crucial stage for identity development, damage to self-esteem during this time can leave long-term emotional scars.

Proposed Solutions

Solution 1: Digital Literacy and Empathy Programs in Schools

Implement mandatory workshops in the school curriculum on "Responsible Digital Citizenship." The focus should not be only on banning technology use, but on teaching students the real impact of their words on screen and how to move from being "passive witnesses" to "active defenders."

Solution 2: Anonymous Reporting Channels and School Psychological Support

Create an internal school app or hotline that is 100% anonymous where students can report cyberbullying cases (their own or others'). This should be accompanied by a rapid response protocol from the school's psychology team to intervene before the problem goes viral.

Solution 3: Parental Control Tools and Family Co-regulation

Develop community campaigns to train parents on using monitoring technology tools (such as time limits and content filters), while also promoting "co-regulation": open dialogue spaces at home where adolescents feel safe to share if something goes wrong on social media without fear of having their phone taken away as punishment.

Part 2: Learning Journal (Logbook Entry #1)

What problem did I choose and why?

We chose the problem of cyberbullying among adolescents. We decided to focus on this because we witness how social media has become essential for our generation, but also how it can become a toxic place. It seems alarming to us that a comment on a screen can destroy a classmate's emotional stability, and we believe it is an urgent issue that schools and families are not fully managing.

What contexts or data influenced my choice?

The current context of hyperconnectivity we live in influenced us greatly, where an adolescent's virtual life is as real and important as their physical life. We also based our choice on data we see daily: the increase in social anxiety among our acquaintances, news about the consequences of digital harassment, and the fact that platforms like TikTok or Instagram make it easy for rumors to spread across the entire school in minutes.

What possible solutions did I identify?

We identified three main courses of action: first, preventive education in classrooms through digital literacy; second, the creation of technological support tools, such as anonymous reporting systems managed by professionals; and third, active and trained involvement of parents so they stop seeing social media as "kids' stuff" and understand the real risks.

What questions or doubts do I have?

When starting this project, we have the following questions:

  • To what extent do schools have the legal right or obligation to intervene in a conflict that occurs on social media outside school hours?
  • How can victims be convinced to report harassment if an adolescent's biggest fear is that speaking up will lead to their phone being confiscated or them being further isolated from the group?
  • What is the real role that social media algorithms play in the spread of this type of violent behavior?
